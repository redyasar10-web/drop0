## HOW TO READ THIS DOCUMENT

The V1 strategy defined what to post. This playbook tells you exactly what to say and how to say it — down to the opening sentence. Nothing here is a template. Every concept is specific to Chariot, 1Nri, Jireh, and the Accra-to-America thesis.

The editorial POV that governs everything:

> **"Fashion is already global. The stores just haven't caught up."**

That's not a tagline for this document. It's the filter. Every post idea below passes through it. If a concept sounds like you're "discovering" African fashion, cut it. If it sounds like you already know and are letting others in, keep it.

## PART 1: FOUNDER LINKEDIN — 12 POST IDEAS

### POST TYPE 1: BUILD UPDATE (3 posts)

Build Updates are the most underrated post type on LinkedIn. Everyone is broadcasting their wins. The founder who shares the messy middle — with specific numbers, not vague inspiration — earns disproportionate trust. Each Build Update should feel like a founder's notebook entry, not a press release.

**Format rules:** 150-300 words. Numbered or single-sentence paragraphs. One specific number per post. End with what you learned, not what you're celebrating.

---

**BUILD UPDATE 1: "The Form Experiment"**

Hook: *I ran a Google Form to validate demand before placing a single wholesale order. Here's what the data actually said.*

Core insight: The pre-order model is only as good as the quality of the demand signal. Chariot's approach — community voting before wholesale purchase — only works if the votes are real. Walking through what the form data revealed (conversion rate from form to deposit, which product categories got the most interest, what surprised you) makes the model legible and trustworthy to everyone reading: potential brand partners, investors, and early customers.

Chariot-specific angle: The product of this post isn't a lesson about Google Forms. It's proof that Chariot doesn't guess. The form IS the model. Showing it in real numbers positions Chariot as data-first in a category that runs almost entirely on instinct and taste.

Draft opening:
> We launched a Google Form to validate demand for our first Chariot drop. Not a landing page, not a Kickstarter — a form. In 72 hours, 47 responses. I want to show you what that data actually looked like and what it told us.

---

**BUILD UPDATE 2: "What the Customs Math Actually Is"**

Hook: *Here's the line-item breakdown of what it costs to legally bring one dress from Accra to New York — and why every number on that list is a reason our model exists.*

Core insight: Most people assume cross-border fashion is mysterious. Show them it isn't. Walk through the landed cost breakdown — export wholesale price, shipping, Section 122 duties, US warehousing, fulfillment, payment processing — and show what the US retail price needs to be to make this work. This post demystifies the model for brands, consumers, and investors simultaneously.

Chariot-specific angle: This post positions Chariot as the founder who did the math that no one else wanted to do. It also makes the pricing feel earned rather than arbitrary when consumers see the US retail number later.

Word count: 200-250 words. Use a simple table or numbered breakdown in the middle of the post.

Draft opening:
> The most common question I get from brands: "Why does my piece need to cost 2x my domestic retail to work in the US?" I'm going to show you exactly why. This is the actual math behind one product in Chariot's first drop.

---

**BUILD UPDATE 3: "What a Brand Partnership Actually Requires"**

Hook: *I'm building a fashion import company from scratch with no US-side inventory yet. Here's what going from 'I love this brand' to 'signed LOI' actually involves.*

Core insight: The operational story of getting a brand to say yes — what due diligence looks like on both sides, what a brand partner needs to feel confident, how the LOI structure works, what Chariot promises in writing — is compelling to everyone who reads LinkedIn: founders who want to know what building actually looks like, investors who want to know if the model is real, and other brands who might consider partnership.

Chariot-specific angle: Authenticity here is the asset. Don't make this sound polished. Make it sound like someone who is figuring out each step as they go, with enough commercial intelligence to navigate it correctly.

Word count: 200-280 words.

Draft opening:
> I've been in conversations with two Ghanaian fashion brands for the past three weeks. I want to share what that process actually looks like — because almost no one talks about the mechanics of signing a first brand partnership when you're a first-time operator.

### POST TYPE 2: THE WHY (3 posts)

The Why posts are not the manifesto. They are specific scenes from the origin story. The emotional truth behind Chariot exists in moments — a specific conversation, a specific piece of clothing, a specific question someone asked. Each Why post should zoom in on one of those moments and let it do the work.

**Format rules:** 200-300 words. First-person throughout. One scene per post. No grand conclusions — let the story land without narration.

---

**THE WHY 1: "The Question That Wouldn't Stop"**

Hook: *Every time I wore something from back home in the US, someone asked where I got it. I never had a good answer. That bothered me.*

Core insight: The "where did you get that?" moment is the experiential foundation of Chariot. This post isn't about business. It's about the specific feeling of being asked that question and not being able to answer it — not because the brand wasn't good enough, but because the infrastructure to buy it didn't exist. That gap is Chariot.

Chariot-specific angle: Name the specific piece. Name where you were when you got asked. The more specific the scene, the more universally it resonates with every member of the diaspora who has felt the same thing.

Draft opening:
> Freshman year. I'm wearing a piece from 1Nri — I'd brought it back from Accra over break. Three people in one week asked me where I got it. I said "Ghana." They said "can I order one?" I didn't have an answer. That was two years ago. I've been building the answer since.

---

**THE WHY 2: "Why I Didn't Build a Marketplace"**

Hook: *The obvious version of this company is a marketplace. I built something different on purpose.*

Core insight: There are already platforms that aggregate international brands for US buyers. Chariot's differentiation is not the catalog — it's the community validation, the retailer-of-record structure, and the founder's personal accountability for every drop. This post explains the model choice as a belief, not a feature.

Chariot-specific angle: Name specific competitors (not to attack them, but to show you've done the analysis). Explain why the community pre-order model isn't just a launch tactic — it's the structural proof that demand exists before inventory risk is taken.

Word count: 250-300 words.

Draft opening:
> There are platforms that already aggregate international fashion for US buyers. I looked at all of them. I didn't build that. Here's why the pre-order model isn't just a strategy — it's the only honest version of this business.

---

**THE WHY 3: "What De Minimis Actually Did to Independent African Brands"**

Hook: *The US government just changed a trade rule most people have never heard of. It wiped out an entire category of cross-border fashion commerce. I built Chariot knowing this was coming.*

Core insight: The de minimis threshold change and Section 122 tariffs aren't background noise — they're the market condition that makes Chariot's model necessary and defensible. Most founders avoid policy posts because they feel dry. But this is the "why now" question answered in personal terms: the window just closed for the direct-to-consumer model, and Chariot is built for what comes after.

Chariot-specific angle: This post positions the founder as someone who read the regulatory environment and built ahead of it, not behind it. That's an investor signal, a brand partner signal, and a consumer trust signal simultaneously.

Draft opening:
> In March 2025, Congress changed the de minimis rule. Small packages from abroad no longer entered duty-free. Most people didn't notice. Every independent African brand that was shipping DTC to US customers absolutely noticed. I want to explain what that change did — and why I designed Chariot specifically to survive it.

### POST TYPE 3: INDUSTRY POV (3 posts)

Industry POV posts establish the founder as someone who knows the structural landscape — not just their own company. These should feel like informed analysis, not opinion pieces. Use numbers where you have them. Cite regulatory realities. Show you've done the reading that most founders in this space haven't.

**Format rules:** 200-280 words. Neutral opening, specific analysis, clear conclusion. Should be shareable by people who have no interest in fashion specifically but care about trade, commerce, or startups.

---

**INDUSTRY POV 1: "The Section 122 Problem Nobody Is Talking About in Fashion"**

Hook: *Section 122 tariffs now apply to fashion imports from West African countries. Almost no one in the fashion media has covered what this actually means for independent brands.*

Core insight: Walk through what Section 122 is, which countries it applies to, what the effective duty rate is for fashion categories, and why independent brands — which lack the margin to absorb duties the way luxury conglomerates do — are disproportionately affected. This is genuinely useful analysis that the founder is uniquely positioned to provide.

Word count: 220-260 words. Include one specific number (e.g., the effective tariff rate for a garment category).

Draft opening:
> Section 122 gives the US President discretionary authority to impose additional tariffs — up to 15% — when the trade deficit hits certain thresholds. In practice, this has hit West African fashion brands with import duties that can reach 30-40% on certain garment categories. Here's what that does to a brand that retails a dress for GH₵400.

---

**INDUSTRY POV 2: "Why West African Fashion Brands Can't Just 'Do DTC' in the US"**

Hook: *The advice most fashion founders give to international brands is 'just launch a Shopify.' That advice is wrong for brands operating out of Accra or Lagos. Here's why.*

Core insight: Break down the five structural barriers to DTC for a West African fashion brand trying to sell to US consumers: 1) no US entity for payment processing 2) customs and duties uncertainty at time of sale 3) 3-6 week shipping from origin 4) no US returns address 5) no local customer support. Each one of these individually could kill the customer experience. Together, they make DTC essentially unworkable without a US-side partner.

Draft opening:
> I've had this conversation three times in the last month: a Ghanaian fashion brand is told to "just do DTC" in the US. I want to explain exactly why that advice, from someone who hasn't done the customs math, is bad advice.

---

**INDUSTRY POV 3: "What the African Fashion Economy Actually Looks Like Right Now"**

Hook: *People talk about 'African fashion' like it's a single thing. In Accra right now, there are brands with 50,000 Instagram followers, sold-out drops, and no path to the US market. That's a structural problem, not a discovery opportunity.*

Core insight: The West African fashion market is already mature at the independent level. Brands like 1Nri and Jireh have established domestic followings, defined product identities, and repeat customers. What they lack is US infrastructure, not US interest. This post reframes the narrative — from "emerging market" to "underserved access point" — which is exactly the framing that makes Chariot's model strategic rather than charitable.

Draft opening:
> 1Nri has been in Accra's creative scene since 2018. They've had sold-out collections. A loyal repeat-customer base. Collaborations with other brands. What they don't have is a New York warehouse. That's the only thing standing between them and a US customer base. That's what Chariot is building.

### POST TYPE 4: DROP ANNOUNCEMENT (3 posts)

Drop Announcement posts have three moments: pre-drop (building anticipation), launch day (converting attention to action), and post-drop recap (community proof and learning). Each has a different job. None of them should read like promotional copy.

**Format rules:** Pre-drop: 150-200 words, curiosity-driven. Launch day: 100-150 words, action-oriented. Post-drop: 200-280 words, reflection and community celebration.

---

**DROP ANNOUNCEMENT 1: Pre-Drop — "The Community Has Already Voted"**

Hook: *In 3 weeks, Chariot's first drop goes live. I want to show you exactly how we got here.*

Core insight: The pre-drop post should document the community validation process — how many people voted, what they voted for, how the community's choice shaped the specific pieces in the drop. This makes the drop feel earned rather than manufactured.

Draft opening:
> Three weeks from today, Chariot's first drop goes live. Before I announce what it is, I want to show you how we got here: 47 form responses, 23 deposits, one brand, one collection. The community didn't just vote — they funded this. Here's what they chose and why.

---

**DROP ANNOUNCEMENT 2: Launch Day**

Hook: *Chariot's first drop is live. 30 units. 14-day window. This is what two years of logistics, paperwork, and community-building looks like.*

Core insight: Launch day is about conversion without desperation. The tone is calm but clear. State what's available, how long the window is, what the customer experience is. One sentence of emotional context. One clear CTA.

Draft opening:
> The first Chariot drop is live as of this morning. 30 units of 1Nri — three pieces, US-specific sizing, duties paid, ships from New York. Pre-order open for 14 days. If you've been following this build, this is what all of it was for. Link in bio.

---

**DROP ANNOUNCEMENT 3: Post-Drop Recap**

Hook: *First Chariot drop: closed. Here's the honest breakdown — what worked, what didn't, and what I'm building next.*

Core insight: The post-drop recap is the most powerful trust-building post in the entire sequence. It treats the community like they're part of the company — not just customers who bought something. Share real numbers (units sold, revenue if comfortable, conversion rate, what didn't sell). Share what you learned. Give the community credit for making it happen.

Draft opening:
> Chariot's first drop closed 72 hours early. 30 units, pre-ordered by 28 people from 9 cities. I want to share everything I learned — including the two things that didn't work and what I'm changing before Drop 2.

## PART 2: FOUNDER TIKTOK/REELS — 12 VIDEO CONCEPTS

These are 30-60 second videos. The founder is on camera. No production tricks needed — a clean background, decent lighting, direct address. The TikTok/Reels audience is the diaspora community and peer network. The LinkedIn audience is investors and press. Same story, different register.

**Universal TikTok rules:**
- First 3 seconds decide everything. The hook must make the viewer feel something immediately — curiosity, recognition, or mild provocation.
- No intros. Never start with "So, I wanted to talk about..." or your name. Start with the hook sentence.
- End with a question or invitation. TikTok rewards comments.

---

**VIDEO 1: "The 'Where Did You Get That?' Video"**

Hook line (first 3 seconds): *"I've been asked 'where did you get that' every time I wear something from Ghana. I built the answer."*

Content: The founder explains — in 45 seconds — the specific experience of wearing something from 1Nri or Jireh in the US and being asked where to buy it. Show the piece if possible. Name the brand. Explain what Chariot is in one sentence. Direct to the form.

B-roll suggestion: Close shot of an actual 1Nri piece — the label, the fabric texture, worn in a casual US setting (a dorm room, a library, a coffee shop).

CTA: "If you already know these brands, the link in my bio is for you."

Why it performs: Recognition trigger for every diaspora member who has had this exact experience. The emotion is specific nostalgia + relief that someone solved the problem.

---

**VIDEO 2: "What the Import Math Actually Is"**

Hook line: *"People ask me how a dress that costs $30 in Accra can cost $150 in New York. Here's the honest math."*

Content: The founder walks through the landed cost breakdown — export wholesale, shipping, duties, warehousing, fulfillment — in real numbers on screen (text overlay or whiteboard). The point is not to defend the price but to make it transparent. Transparency at this level is almost never done in fashion and will stand out.

B-roll suggestion: The founder writing numbers on a whiteboard or sticky notes, close enough to read.

CTA: "If this model makes sense to you, pre-orders open [date]."

Why it performs: Transparency is disarming. People who would have assumed price gouging become brand defenders once they understand the math. Information as trust-building.

---

**VIDEO 3: "What Accra's Creative Scene Actually Looks Like Right Now"**

Hook line: *"People in New York are just now finding out about brands that Accra's creative class has been wearing for six years."*

Content: The founder describes — without exoticizing — the fashion scene in Accra: the brands, the venues, the customer profile, the level of sophistication. Frame it as a city that runs on its own cultural logic and doesn't need US validation. Chariot is just the shipping mechanism.

B-roll suggestion: Images from 1Nri's or Jireh's Accra campaigns (with permission). Real street contexts, not staged tourism.

CTA: "We're bringing two of those brands to the US through community pre-order. Form is in the bio."

Why it performs: Reframes the diaspora community's relationship to the origin culture — from nostalgic to current. Taps into cultural pride without sentimentality.

---

**VIDEO 4: "Why This Isn't Dropshipping"**

Hook line: *"People keep calling Chariot a dropshipping business. It's the opposite. Here's the difference."*

Content: The founder explains the structural distinction: dropshipping ships from origin per order, Chariot buys wholesale after demand is validated and ships from a US location. This means faster delivery, no customs surprise for the buyer, free returns that are actually feasible, and a US-side customer service experience. The point isn't to defend the model — it's to explain why the customer experience is materially different.

B-roll suggestion: Founder with a physical package — a branded Chariot box, a hangtag — to make the operational reality visual.

CTA: "If you want to understand the model fully, I break it down in my pinned post."

Why it performs: Addresses skepticism before it becomes an objection. Everyone who watches is either reassured or educated.

---

**VIDEO 5: "De Minimis Explained in 45 Seconds"**

Hook line: *"A rule most people have never heard of just killed a specific type of cross-border fashion business. I built Chariot knowing this was coming."*

Content: Explain de minimis (packages under $800 used to enter the US duty-free, enabling DTC from international brands at low shipping costs). Explain that this changed, and what it did to brands that were relying on it. Explain that Chariot's model doesn't rely on de minimis — it operates as importer of record, with duties built into the price upfront.

B-roll suggestion: The founder at a laptop with a headline article about the de minimis change visible on screen.

CTA: "This is why the model matters. Drop 1 opens in two weeks."

Why it performs: Positions the founder as someone who understands structural forces, not just aesthetics. Investors and press will share this. The community will trust it.

Content: The founder shows the Google Form (or a screen recording of it), explains the community voting mechanic, shares the top voted choices, and reveals that the form responses also included deposits — meaning the demand was financially real before any wholesale order was placed.

## PART 4: THE VOICE DISTINCTION

@chariotarchive and the founder's personal channel say Chariot-true things in fundamentally different registers. Mixing them is the most common content mistake for founder-led brands. Here's the precise distinction and how to apply it.

### The Core Difference

**Founder voice:** "I" is present. The story is in progress. Vulnerability is permitted and intentional. The founder is figuring something out in public and inviting the audience to watch.

**@chariotarchive voice:** "I" does not exist. The brand speaks from a position of arrival, not becoming. It does not explain itself. It does not hedge. It does not invite you to watch it become something. It already is.

Think of it this way: the founder is building the house. @chariotarchive is the house. The founder shows you the beams and the tools. The account shows you the rooms.

### The Five Voice Axes

| Dimension | Founder Channel | @chariotarchive |
|---|---|---|
| Perspective | First-person, building-in-progress | Third-person editorial, complete-state |
| Time | Present tense of process | Present tense of authority |
| Transparency | Shares what isn't working | Never acknowledges uncertainty |
| Emotion | Allowed, and specific | Controlled, and deployed deliberately |
| Explanation | Explains the model | Assumes you already know |

---

### 5 Example Sentence Pairs

**1. On the 1Nri brand**

Founder: "I spent an afternoon going through 1Nri's full catalog trying to figure out which pieces would translate for a US customer who doesn't know Accra yet. The Dry Bones tee made it obvious."

@chariotarchive: "1Nri's Dry Bones tee has been in rotation in Accra since 2022. It sells out every time."

---

**2. On the pricing**

Founder: "A dress that costs GH₵400 in Accra retails at $145 through Chariot. I'm going to show you every line item that makes that math necessary — because I think you deserve to understand it before you pre-order."

@chariotarchive: "Designed in Accra. Retails at $145 in the US. Duties included. Ships from New York."

---

**3. On the community vote**

Founder: "We got 47 form responses in the first 72 hours. I didn't expect that. I want to tell you what that number meant to me at 11pm on a Tuesday and what it told me about demand validation."

@chariotarchive: "The community voted. We ordered. Drop 1 opens [date]."

---

**4. On the launch strategy**

Founder: "I'm not running ads. I'm going to university GSA chapters because that's where the people who already know these brands are. It's a slower start and a much better foundation."

@chariotarchive: "Community chosen. Community shipped. Already wearing it."

---

**5. On the cultural significance**

Founder: "I grew up going back to Ghana and watching the creative scene there evolve into something that has no equivalent in US retail. That's a market failure, not a cultural gap. Chariot is built around that observation."

@chariotarchive: "What the rest of the world is already wearing. The stores just haven't caught up."

The founder posts about the problem, the model, and the mission without needing to name a specific partner. When the LOI announcement comes, the audience already knows what it means.

### Days 4-7: Establish the Model

**LinkedIn Post (Day 4):** The "Why this isn't dropshipping" / "Why I didn't build a marketplace" post (The Why 2 from Part 1). The model is the content.

### Days 11-14: Create the Anticipation State

**LinkedIn Post (Day 11):** A short, specific post: "Chariot's first brand partnership is close to signed. Before I announce it, I want to tell you what the evaluation process looked like." ## APPENDIX: QUICK REFERENCE — VOICE RULES

**Things that are always true in Chariot's voice:**
- Name the city of origin (Accra, not "West Africa")
- Name the brand (1Nri or Jireh, not "our partner brand")
- State operational facts as reassurances, never as features ("duties paid" not "we cover duty fees as a service")
- Use specific numbers when you have them
- Trust the reader — never explain the obvious
- "Already wearing it" is the correct sign-off for @chariotarchive

**Things that are never true in Chariot's voice:**
- "Redefining," "bridging," "celebrating," "empowering," "incredible," "stunning"
- "African fashion" as a genre or category
- "Discover" or "explore" (we are not a travel company)
- Centering the US as the validation point for quality that already exists elsewhere
- Exclamation marks in @chariotarchive copy
- Urgency language that is manufactured rather than factual

**The test for every piece of content:**
Read it aloud. If it sounds like it could be from a different brand in a different category, rewrite it. Chariot's voice should be specific enough that nothing it says could be said by anyone else.
