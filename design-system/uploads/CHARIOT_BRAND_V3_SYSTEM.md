### Color System V4

Eight colors, four tiers. Warm monochromatic family (all hues 17-38°) with two chromatic accents. Every neutral shares a yellow-brown undertone — the palette photographs together, prints together, and reads as designed rather than assembled.

**The Eight-Color System:**

| Tier | Name | Hex | Pantone | Role |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Foundation | **Ink** | `#111111` | Black 6 C | Wordmark, all body text, authority elements. Not pure black — warm printing-ink dark. |
| Foundation | **Harmattan** | `#F2EBE0` | 9183 C | Signature background. Named for the Saharan wind that coats West Africa amber-warm every dry season. |
| Neutral | **Dust** | `#E4DDD4` | 9144 C | Secondary surfaces, card backgrounds. The layer between Harmattan and Ash. |
| Neutral | **Ash** | `#C8C0B5` | 9186 C | Borders, dividers, inactive states. Structural only — never a background fill. |
| Neutral | **Shadow** | `#5C5449` | 439 C | Secondary text, captions, metadata. Warm dark mid-tone for subordinate copy. |
| Signal | **Kente** | `#C9921E` | 7409 C | Brand partner names, drop badges, hero accent. Earns power by rarity — max one instance per screen section. |
| Signal | **Laterite** | `#9B4523` | 7526 C | Campaign urgency, closing states, sold-out. Named for West African red iron soil. Never paired with Kente. |
| Functional | **Salt** | `#FAFAF8` | 9161 C | Reversed type on dark, print base, accessibility edge cases. Warm optical white. |

**Contrast ratios (WCAG 2.1):**
- Ink on Harmattan: **16.7:1** — AAA ✓
- Shadow on Harmattan: **5.7:1** — AA ✓
- Salt on Ink: **19.5:1** — AAA ✓
- Kente on Harmattan: **2.8:1** — headlines/badges only (18pt+), never body copy

**Rules that do not bend:**
- Page backgrounds: Harmattan. Secondary surfaces: Dust. Never Ash as a fill.
- Body text: Ink. Secondary/metadata text: Shadow. Reversed text: Salt on Ink.
- Kente is a headline and badge color only — insufficient contrast for body text.
- Kente and Laterite never appear together on the same surface.
- No pure `#000000` or pure `#ffffff` anywhere — use Ink and Salt respectively.
- No cold blues, greys, greens, or purples. The palette is warm throughout.
- No Pan-African red/black/green as system colors.

### Typography System V3

The previous direction (Founders Grotesk + Canela) is correct but needs sharper execution rules.

| Role | Typeface | Voice |
|---|---|---|
| Wordmark + Hero Display | **ABC Monument Grotesk** (or Söhne as backup) | Swiss geometric. Reads like a masthead, not a startup. Used by The Cut. Ageless. |
| Editorial Body | **Freight Text Pro** (or Spectral as free alternative) | A warm, slightly literary serif. Feels written, not designed. Earns trust in long-form copy. |
| Operational / Labels | **Söhne Mono** (or DM Mono) | Price tags. SKU numbers. Trust signals. "Ships in 3 days." Precision, not decoration. |

**Typography rules:**
- Display type: 5 words or fewer per headline. If you need more, break it into two lines.
- All body copy: left-aligned. Never centered for blocks of text.
- Drop announcements: Display + Kente color for brand name.
- Trust signals (price, shipping, return policy): always Mono.
- Founders' names: italic Freight Text.
- Never bold everything. Bold is a decision, not a default.

### Logo System V3

**The Mark:** CHARIOT in Monument Grotesk, tracked out at +80 letterspacing. Uppercase only. The name is the mark.

**The Stamp:** A condensed version for garment tags, embossing, wax seals: the wordmark in a tight rectangle with a thin hairline border. Think: customs declaration stamp. Because that's exactly what Chariot is — the entity that stamps something as cleared, approved, delivered.

**What the logo must survive:** Small on an Instagram story. Embossed on a tissue paper wrap. Printed in Ink on a Harmattan hangtag. If it doesn't work in all three, it's not done.

**What it must never be:**
- An African mask, shield, Adinkra symbol, map outline, or any African motif
- A literal chariot, horse, or wheel
- Any icon that would feel out of place on a Parisian concept store website

### Photography Direction V3

**The "Transit" Principle:** Every Chariot photograph exists between two cities. The garment is from Accra. The photograph is from New York. The model looks at home in both. The image never explains itself.

**Product Detail Shots:**
- Macro textures: weave, topstitching, button construction, fabric fall
- Warm backgrounds only: linen, raw concrete, oiled wood, unbleached paper
- No white lightbox. Ever.
- Lighting: raking side-light that shows texture, or golden hour natural

**Campaign / Editorial:**
- Models: Black, natural hair, full range of complexions and body types
- Environments: US creative life — a studio in Bed-Stuy, a gallery in Ponce City Market, a morning kitchen in Logan Circle. Never safari. Never marketplace. Never tourist Africa.
- No smiling-in-the-product-shot energy. Fashion photography. Subject is in their own world.
- One environmental anchor per image: one piece of furniture, one architecture element, one plant. Not styled within an inch of its life.

**The four shots every product needs:**
1. Full garment, flat lay, Harmattan background — the reference shot
2. On body, facing away or 3/4 view — shows fit and scale
3. Macro detail — shows craft and justifies price
4. In context — worn in a real environment by a real person doing something

### Brand Voice V3

The previous voice rules are correct. These additions make them sharper:

**The Two Voices Chariot Must Never Blend:**

*Voice A — The Channel (social, product pages, community):*
Knows things before everyone else. Speaks with the confidence of someone who has already been there. Never announces. Never explains. Shows.

*Voice B — The Founder (personal LinkedIn, TikTok/Reels, direct outreach):*
Transparent about the build. Shares what didn't work. Earns trust through honesty, not polish. The only place Chariot can say "I" instead of "we."

**The Voice Axes:**

| IS | IS NOT |
|---|---|
| Specific (names places, people, fabrics) | Generic ("African fashion," "unique design") |
| Insider authority | Tourist excitement |
| Operationally confident | Process-obsessed |
| Culturally literate | Performatively diverse |
| Calm | Urgent |
| Editorial | Sales pitch |

**Banned words and phrases (permanent):**
- "Redefining" — used by every brand that has never redefined anything
- "Bridging worlds" / "bridging the gap" — sounds like a TED talk
- "Empowering" / "celebrating" / "honoring" — tells not shows
- "Discover" / "explore" — positions us as a travel agency
- "African fashion" as a category — we are not a genre, we are specific brands
- "Stunning" / "beautiful" / "incredible" — describe, don't praise

**Copy example — before and after:**

❌ "We're celebrating the incredible designs of African fashion by bridging the gap between African designers and American consumers. Discover stunning pieces today."

✓ "1Nri has been dressing Accra's creative class for six years. Their US debut is here. One drop. 14 days. No customs chaos."

## PART 2: THE SUPREME PLAYBOOK — CHARIOT EDITION

Supreme didn't build a brand. They built a **world** — and they let specific people into that world first.

The original Supreme customers weren't told they were in a test. They were told they were **the real ones.** The people who understood street culture before it had a name. Supreme gave them access, they wore it, and the world noticed. The brand never had to explain itself because the community did it for them.

Chariot runs the same play. Different world. Same psychology.

---

### What the Supreme Playbook Actually Is

Five interlocking psychological mechanisms, running simultaneously:

**1. Identity Before Product**
Supreme customers weren't buying hoodies. They were buying membership in a tribe that had taste before taste was mainstream. For Chariot: customers aren't buying a dress. They are the people who already know 1Nri. They bought into that identity before Chariot existed. Chariot just makes it purchasable.

**2. Scarcity as Social Signal**
Supreme's drops were never about the quantity. They were about the queue — the visible proof that people want this. A 2-week pre-order window with a hard cap creates the same signal. "We only ordered 30 of these because 30 people in the community wanted them."

**3. Community as Gatekeeping**
You can't buy from Supreme through a friend. You can't re-sell before it sells out (officially). The barrier to entry is cultural literacy, not price. For Chariot: the Google Form is not a marketing funnel. It is an **initiation**. Filling it out proves you know what you're looking for.

**4. The Founder Channel IS the Brand**
Supreme's James Jebbia never did interviews. The mystique was the brand. For Chariot, the opposite is true — the founder *is* the voice, because the brand is still becoming real. Post on LinkedIn and TikTok as the founder before @chariotarchive has momentum. The community forms around a person they trust before they trust a brand.

**5. Exclusivity is Framed as Inclusion**
Supreme makes you feel included in something exclusive. Not excluded from something popular. The difference is framing: "We chose to work with people who already know the brands" not "you can only buy this if you're in the right circles." Chariot's community is for anyone who already knows — the door is open to anyone who has the cultural context. Which is exactly the African diaspora.

### The Three-Phase Launch Architecture

**Phase 1: Inner Circle (Weeks 1-4)**
*Goal: 5-20 founding depositors. No public launch.*

This phase doesn't happen on Instagram. It happens in DMs, group chats, and after-class conversations. The ambassadors are operating here. The goal is not awareness — it is conversion. Every person in Phase 1 is a founding member who puts real money down.

Who gets access: Only people the ambassadors can personally vouch for.

What happens: They fill the Google Form. They get a personal follow-up from the ambassador or the founder within 24 hours. When the drop opens, they get 24-hour early access before any public announcement.

**Phase 2: Community Expansion (Weeks 5-10)**
*Goal: 50+ form responses, 20+ deposits. GSA/ASA reach activated.*

This is when the ambassador network expands from "people I know" to "communities I'm in." GSA/ASA group chats, university networks, diaspora community spaces. The Google Form link goes into these spaces from trusted people — not cold.

Instagram starts during Phase 2, but only brand content (not promotional). The account exists as proof, not as acquisition.

**Phase 3: Drop Announcement (Weeks 11-14)**
*Goal: 14-day pre-order window. Public for the first time.*

First @chariotarchive drop announcement. Founder LinkedIn/TikTok post. First public content. The community that already exists becomes the social proof that makes the public announcement credible.

The drop counter is visible: "47 of 50 units reserved." This is the Zeigarnik Effect + Scarcity Heuristic in one number.

## PART 3: AMBASSADOR STRATEGY — COMPLETE GUIDE

### The Core Psychology Problem (And Its Solution)

**The problem:** If someone perceives that they're being asked to pay for something a peer is building, the psychology reverses. It triggers three negative responses simultaneously:
- *Skepticism* ("This person is using me to fund their project")
- *Status-deflation* ("Why would I buy from someone in my same position?")
- *Discomfort* ("This person is profiting off my community")

None of these are rational. All of them are real.

**The solution — not hiding the truth, but changing what's visible:**

The ambassadors are **community members who found something real and are bringing it to people who would care about it.** They are not promoting a business they built. They are sharing access they have.

The distinction is not a lie. It is literally true. The ambassador found Chariot (through the founder's personal outreach) and decided it was worth bringing to people they know. That is what they are doing. The mechanics of compensation and the founder's student status are irrelevant to the person who gets a text message about a brand they love from someone they trust.

**What creates authority instead:**

- The *brands* (1Nri, Jireh) have authority. They are real, established, respected. The ambassador is the access mechanism to something that already matters.
- The *community vote mechanism* creates perceived legitimacy. "The community decides what gets ordered" means this isn't one person's speculation — it's collective demand made real.
- The *operational story* creates confidence. "Ships from New York, no customs, free returns" sounds like infrastructure, not a student hustle.

**The one rule:** Ambassadors never say who built Chariot or describe their relationship to the founder. If asked directly, they say "I'm one of the founding members — I just make sure the right people know about it."

---

### The Three Conversation Registers

**Register 1: Close Friends (DM / iMessage — 1-to-1)**

The most personal. No pitch energy. This is a text from a friend.

> "Okay so this might be random but do you know 1Nri? The brand from Accra? I found a way to actually get their stuff shipped here with no customs nightmare. It's this platform called Chariot — community decides what gets ordered, they handle all the import stuff. I got early founding member access and I can share the link. Takes like 2 minutes to fill out. You want in?"

*What this does:*
- "do you know 1Nri?" — tests cultural literacy. If they don't know, the conversation either ends or becomes an education moment. If they do know, you've already won.
- "I found a way" — ambassador is a discoverer, not a promoter
- "I got early access and I can share the link" — implies exclusivity granted, not sold
- "You want in?" — the only close you ever need

**Register 2: Trusted Group Chat (GSA/ASA/community channels)**

More structured. Uses the social proof of the group itself.

> "Dropping this here because I know people in this chat actually care. Anyone who knows the brands coming out of Accra right now — 1Nri, Jireh — there's finally a way to get them here without the customs situation. Platform called Chariot. Community votes on what gets ordered, they handle the import. I've been part of it since early and it's the real thing. Link to vote on the first drop: [link]. First people to fill it out get founding member early access — they notify you before the drop goes public."

*What this does:*
- "people in this chat actually care" — qualifies the audience and flatters them
- "finally a way" — acknowledges the existing pain (the customs chaos everyone has felt)
- "I've been part of it since early" — social proof from someone inside the group
- "founding member early access" — creates urgency without lying about it

**Register 3: Wider Network (Friend-of-friend / university club, LinkedIn DM)**

Less personal assumption. The cultural hook has to earn its way.

> "Hey [Name] — I don't know if you follow the fashion scene coming out of Accra right now, but the brands there (1Nri, Jireh) have been dressing a whole creative class that most people in the US have never heard of. I'm connected to a platform that's doing the first US drop — community pre-order model, no customs fees, ships from New York. They're in early access mode and I can get you in before it goes public. If that sounds interesting, I can send you the link."

*What this does:*
- "I don't know if you follow" — low pressure, gives them an out if not relevant
- "dressing a whole creative class" — positions the brands as culturally significant before asking anything
- "I'm connected to" — connected, not building, not promoting
- "I can get you in" — ambassador is the access, not Chariot

### Behaviors That Build Community

These are not scripted. These are things the ambassador *does* that accumulate social trust over weeks.

**Wear the brand publicly and be asked about it.**
This is the original play. The ambassador wears 1Nri or Jireh to class, to events, anywhere. When someone asks "where did you get that?" — that is the entire Chariot conversation in one moment. The answer is never "Chariot." The answer is: "[Brand name]. You can get it here actually — let me send you the link." Chariot is how you got it here. The brand is what you're wearing.

**Post authentically about the cultural context, not the product.**
Instagram stories, group chat context. Not "check out this drop." Instead: "Saw this 1Nri piece in a photo from Lagos in January. This is what's happening over there." Cultural credibility building. Chariot as discovery mechanism, not marketing channel.

**Be the first to fill out the form and screenshot it.**
"Just voted on the first Chariot drop. They're trying to get [specific piece] here. If enough people vote for it they'll import it." This is public commitment that others can match.

**Tell the origin story, not the Chariot story.**
"I was at [event/gathering] and someone had this piece from 1Nri on and everyone kept stopping them. That's literally why this exists." The personal observation moment is more powerful than any brand narrative.

---

### What Ambassadors NEVER Say

These kill the brand and the ambassador's credibility simultaneously:

| Never Say | Why |
|---|---|
| "My friend built this" / "Someone I know made this" | Triggers student-distrust psychology |
| "I'm an ambassador for Chariot" | Reveals compensation, kills authenticity |
| "I get paid for this" / "I get a commission" | Same problem, more direct |
| "We're building this" | "We" puts them on the founding team — kills the "I found this" frame |
| "It's a startup" / "They're early stage" | Startup signals risk. Use "platform" or just describe what it does. |
| "They're trying to get the word out" | Makes it sound like they need help — authority collapse |
| "It's like [competitor]" | Never compare to anything. Let it stand alone. |
| Any price framing in first message | The price comes after desire is established, not before |

---

### The Follow-Up Protocol

Most people won't convert on the first message. This is expected and fine.

**24 hours later — if no response:**
No second message. Let it breathe. The scarcity of the follow-up is a signal.

**3 days later — if they opened but didn't fill the form:**
> "Hey — the drop is filling up. Just FYI."

That's it. No pressure. The information (filling up) does the work.

**If they say "what is this actually?":**
This is the warm signal. Now you give them the full context:
> "It's a pre-order platform for independent West African fashion brands. The community votes on what gets imported, Chariot handles all the customs and shipping, and it ships from New York. First drop is 1Nri and Jireh — both out of Accra. I've been following these brands for a while and this is the first time I've seen a real way to get them here."

**If they ask "who started it?":**
> "I'm honestly not sure of all the details of the founding — I'm one of the early community members. What I know is the logistics work and the brands are real. That's why I'm in it."

This is honest. The ambassador is a community member who doesn't necessarily know the founders' CVs. No lie needed.

---

### Ambassador Self-Identity: The Reframe

The ambassador must feel this, not just say it:

**They are not a promoter.** Promoters are paid to talk about things they may or may not believe in. Their credibility is borrowed.

**They are a cultural gatekeeper.** They know which brands matter in Accra right now. They know why 1Nri's construction is different. They know the customs nightmare is real because they or their family has experienced it. They are sharing something that already has value with people who are already positioned to want it.

The compensation ($10 per signup, $50 per deposit) is not why they do it. It is incidental to the fact that they are sharing something real. If they frame it as compensation-motivated even in their own head, the conversations will feel wrong. Frame it as: "I'm getting paid to do something I'd do anyway."

## PART 4: COMMUNITY IDENTITY ARCHITECTURE

*From the community-marketing framework: The strongest communities are built around who members ARE, not around a product.*

### Who the Chariot Community Member Is

They are not a "fashion buyer." They are not an "African diaspora consumer."

They are: **The person who already knew.**

They already knew 1Nri before Chariot. They already had the frustration of not being able to get the brand here. They already had the conversation where they told someone about a piece from back home. Chariot found them, not the other way around.

**The identity the community reinforces:**
> "I am someone with taste that most people in the US haven't caught up to yet. I don't wait for American retail to tell me what to want. I already know."

This is a status identity. It is powerful. It is also true — members of the African diaspora genuinely carry cultural knowledge about these brands that the US has not integrated. The community makes that knowledge visible and actionable.

---

### The Community Flywheel for Chariot

```
Member discovers Chariot through someone they trust
        ↓
Member fills Google Form (first commitment act)
        ↓
Member receives drop notification (they feel prioritized)
        ↓
Member deposits (real money = real commitment)
        ↓
Member receives the piece (physical artifact of belonging)
        ↓
Member is asked "where did you get that?" in real life
        ↓
Member becomes the ambassador — the flywheel closes
```

Every person who receives a Chariot piece and gets asked about it is a living advertisement and a potential ambassador. The product, worn publicly, generates the original "where did you get that?" question that started everything.

**Design for the flywheel from day one.** Every decision should ask: does this accelerate the loop or slow it down?

---

### The Founding Members Frame

The first 20-50 people in the community are not early adopters. They are **founding members.** This distinction is not marketing language — it changes the psychological contract.

An early adopter tries something and leaves if it doesn't work out.

A founding member helped build something. Their opinions shaped it. Their vote determined what got ordered. They have the IKEA Effect — they invested effort, so they feel ownership.

**The Founding Member Program:**
- Access to every drop 24 hours before public
- Their vote counts double in drop selection (framed as recognition, not manipulation)
- Name in the "Founding Chapter" on the Chariot about page (when it exists)
- A physical token: a Chariot customs stamp card, sent with their first order

This costs almost nothing. The psychological return is enormous.
